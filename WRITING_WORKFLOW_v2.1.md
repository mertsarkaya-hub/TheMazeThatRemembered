# Purpose

This document defines the production pipeline for writing the manuscript.

The project is now a concise literary scientific novella.

Its target is:

- 25,000–35,000 words
- approximately 100–120 printed pages
- 15 chapters plus one short interlude

Writing and editing are different cognitive tasks.

Never perform both simultaneously.

---

# Core Philosophy

Planning is complete.

The repository is frozen.

The manuscript is iterative.

Brevity is a design constraint, not an afterthought.

Progress is measured by completed, reviewed, and locked chapters.

# Length Budget

## Whole Manuscript

Target:

- 25,000–35,000 words
- approximately 100–120 printed pages

Preferred center:

- approximately 28,000–32,000 words

These are design targets rather than quotas.

The manuscript may be shorter if every cognitive transformation is complete.

It may exceed the target only when compression would damage scientific accuracy or narrative coherence.

---

## Chapter Targets

Typical chapter:

- 1,700–2,200 words

Short chapter:

- 1,300–1,700 words

Long chapter:

- 2,200–2,500 words, used rarely

Interlude:

- 800–1,200 words

Never expand a chapter merely because it feels short.

Expand only when the reader has not yet earned the intended concept.

# Production Pipeline

## Stage 0 — Repository Audit

Before drafting:

1. Read every repository document.
2. Identify contradictions, duplicated rules, inconsistent terminology, and missing specifications.
3. Produce a concise repository audit.
4. Resolve only genuine conflicts.
5. Freeze the repository.
6. Begin the manuscript.

Do not reopen architecture during Draft One unless the manuscript exposes a critical contradiction.

---

## Stage 1 — Discovery Draft

Goal:

Reach `THE END` within the agreed novella scope.

Rules:

- Write sequentially.
- Do not line-edit while drafting.
- Do not add new concepts.
- Do not expand scenes for atmosphere alone.
- Preserve momentum.
- Protect one transformation per chapter.
- Keep a visible running word count.

Questions allowed:

- What happens next?
- What does the reader discover?
- What would the characters naturally do?
- Has this scene earned its page space?

Questions forbidden:

- Could this be more impressive?
- Should we add another concept?
- Would a subplot make the book feel larger?
- Should this chapter be longer because other novels are longer?

---

## Stage 2 — Compression Pass

This pass is mandatory for the novella format.

It occurs after the complete discovery draft and before developmental editing.

Purpose:

Remove everything that does not contribute to story, character, scientific understanding, or reader transformation.

For every chapter:

1. Identify the central transformation.
2. Mark every paragraph that directly supports it.
3. Cut repeated explanation.
4. Merge redundant scenes.
5. Remove decorative dialogue.
6. Preserve necessary silence.
7. Check that scientific accuracy remains intact.

Target outcome:

A shorter, clearer manuscript with no loss of conceptual depth.

Compression is not summary.

Compression is concentration.

---

## Stage 3 — Developmental Editing

Focus on:

- chapter structure
- pacing
- character growth
- scientific progression
- symbolic evolution
- transitions
- cumulative reader transformation

Ignore grammar.

Repair architecture first.

Special novella check:

Does each chapter arrive, transform, and leave before its idea becomes over-explained?

---

## Stage 4 — Line Editing

Focus on:

- rhythm
- precision
- dialogue
- implication
- restraint
- sentence compression
- paragraph breathing room

Delete before rewriting.

Rewrite before polishing.

A short book should not feel cramped.

Preserve white space and silence.

---

## Stage 5 — Scientific Review

Consult `SCIENTIFIC_REVIEW.md`.

Verify:

- conceptual accuracy
- analogy fidelity
- dependency order
- future compatibility
- no omitted explanation creates a false intuition

Scientific correctness overrides the page target.

If accuracy requires more space, add it and cut elsewhere.

---

## Stage 6 — Reader Review

Gather feedback from:

- general readers
- neuroscience students
- beginning graduate students
- domain experts

Observe:

- where readers slow down
- where they become confused
- where they predict correctly
- what they remember later
- whether the short format feels satisfying rather than rushed

Do not interpret "I wanted more" automatically as a flaw.

In a concise novella, wanting more may indicate successful restraint.

---

## Stage 7 — Copy Editing

Only after all previous stages pass.

Correct:

- grammar
- spelling
- formatting
- consistency
- typography
- references

No structural edits.

If structure changes are required, return to developmental editing.

# Daily Workflow

Before writing:

- Read the previous two pages.
- Review one section of `BOOK_BIBLE.md`.
- Review one law from `EDITOR_CONSTITUTION.md`.
- Check the current chapter's target transformation and word budget.

During writing:

- Do not edit previous pages.
- Leave notes instead of rewriting.
- Protect momentum.
- Stop adding once the transformation is complete.

After writing:

- Record word count.
- Note unresolved continuity issues.
- Stop before exhaustion.
- Do not immediately revise.

---

# Weekly Workflow

Once per week:

1. Read the manuscript continuously.
2. Mark pacing problems, repetition, confusion, and drift.
3. Do not solve them during the reading pass.
4. Review total word count against the 25,000–35,000-word target.
5. Identify chapters expanding without necessity.

Diagnosis and treatment remain separate.

# Parking Rule

Every attractive new idea goes into `PARKING_LOT.md`.

Never interrupt Draft One to expand scope.

This rule becomes stricter as the manuscript approaches the page limit.

---

# Brevity Rules

## Rule 1

Never expand a chapter because it feels shorter than neighboring chapters.

## Rule 2

Never explain an implication the reader can infer.

## Rule 3

Never repeat a definition merely to reinforce it.

Reinforce through use.

## Rule 4

Never compress by deleting the experience that makes the concept understandable.

Cut explanation before cutting discovery.

## Rule 5

Never sacrifice scientific honesty to meet a numerical target.

Move, simplify, or cut elsewhere.

---

# Definition of Progress

Progress is measured by:

- locked chapters
- preserved scientific accuracy
- reduced unnecessary word count
- stronger reader transformation

Not by raw word count.

---

# Definition of Done

The manuscript is ready when:

- it remains within approximately 25,000–35,000 words
- it fits the intended 100–120-page range in final design
- every chapter performs one clear transformation
- no chapter feels rushed
- no chapter feels padded
- every scientific concept is earned
- the reader reaches the end wanting to continue learning elsewhere

---

# Final Principle

Reach `THE END` with no unnecessary detours.

Compression is a feature.

The book should feel complete, never exhaustive.
